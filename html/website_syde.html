What is Systems Design Engineering?

"What even is Systems?" - That's the running joke among students in SYDE. Very often we hear, "S. Y. D. E. What is Systems don't ask me!" all throughout the Systems Design Department. It can seem like nobody, not even Systems Design students, knows what Systems Design actually is. The name itself is vague, much so that parents and incoming students ask, "What is Systems Design?", or "What kind of systems do you work with?", "Is it a new program?", or even, "Is it like, Computer Systems?", along with the common, "Hi there, where's the (insert other engineering program here) booth?".

It turns out that everyone has a different point of view on what Systems Design actually is. The nature of the program allows for each and every one of its students the ability to shape the program for themselves. It is one of the only programs of its kind in Canada; no other university has anything that resembles SYDE or provide the skills that SYDE students learn. After talking to classmates, upper years, and graduates, below is a commonly used way to describe what SYDE is, to someone who wants to know more about it.

Systems Design Engineering is the modelling of interdisciplinary branches of engineering, used to solve real world problems using the design process combined with a unique user centric design approach.

Systems Design Engineering is a multidisciplinary, jack-of-all-trades, big-picture type of engineering, that focuses on solving an identified problem using a hollistic approach, through the modelling of systems and the implementation of the design process. Systems Design Engineering can be broken down into 2 parts: the systems part, and the design part. The Systems part refers to the technical skills that we learn in our lower years. We learn a bit of mechanical, electrical, computer, material, chemical, and software engineering to build up our skillset and to have a fundamental understanding of every single engineering branch. In our upper years, we learn the math to put it all together to be able to model the system of different parts. The Design part is SYDE's speciality. Our Design is focused on user centric design, where we learn about the overall design process and how to approach a real world problem. We learn about identifying and analyzing the overall problem, collecting and modelling data, and using our gathered research to effectively generate a solution to the problem we have established. Since we work with real life problems, we are required to analyze our solution to see if what we proposed truly addresses the problem and whether or not it has any negative side effects. If so, we start the design process over again. If we combine the technical skills and modelling of the Systems part, and combine it with the user centric design part, together, you have Systems Design Engineering.

Major credits to Laura (SYDE 2019), who derived this descriptive definition.

SYDE arguably has one of the tighest communities within the Waterloo Engineering bubble. SYDE has a cohort system, so we are with the people who are in the same stream as us throughout our undergrad career. Our "SYDE family" as most people would call it, refers to the upper year and lower year SYDE student relationships. The lower year on-stream SYDE students are often referred to as "SYDE babies", or even "SYDE grandbabies" by the graduating class. Upper year SYDE students are known to help transition 1A SYDE students into univeristy life, and serve as their mentors. In my 1A year, our upper years (the 2Bs) decorated our classroom, writing "Welcome SYDE 2023" alongside putting up streamers and balloons. We were then matched to a 2B SYDE mentor and a 4A mentor, to whom we can ask for guidance anytime. We also host SYDE + BME exclusive events every term, the biggest one being Coffee House. The entire SYDE community is so supportive, and I am truly happy to have experienced it through my undergrad.

With the way that our classes are structured, first year SYDE students have the exact same schedule as one another (with the exception of certain labs and tutorials). Our classes are also exclusive, as we don't take any classes with other engineerings (Despite SYDE and Biomedical Engineering taking the same math courses in 1A, we have separate lectures for each section, with SYDE being in one and BME in the other). This is a great way to ensure you have friends throughout at the start of your undergrad as you all have something in common.

The SYDE faculty now lives on the 6th floor of E5 and E7. We tend to stay in a single classroom each term. In first year, we mainly stay in E5 6008, and we move around to other classrooms as well. As the terms go by, we would stay in E5 6004 or E5 6006. Since we're all in the same class and the same classroom, it ends up fostering a great community and it's really convinent during the winter terms.
